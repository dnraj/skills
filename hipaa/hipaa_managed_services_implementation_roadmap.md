# HIPAA Implementation Roadmap and Control Matrix for a Managed Services Company

This document provides a practical, ground-up HIPAA compliance implementation approach for a managed services company that may create, receive, maintain, transmit, support, monitor, back up, or otherwise access PHI/ePHI for covered entity or business associate customers. It is designed for MSPs, MSSPs, cloud operations providers, help desk providers, backup and disaster recovery providers, endpoint managers, monitoring providers, and customer-environment administrators.

## Important scope assumptions

- **Entity role**: The managed services company is assumed to be a HIPAA business associate or subcontractor when it provides services involving access to PHI/ePHI on behalf of healthcare customers or another business associate. HHS states that HIPAA Rules apply to covered entities and business associates, and that business associates may be directly liable for certain HIPAA requirements ([HHS Covered Entities and Business Associates](https://www.hhs.gov/hipaa/for-professionals/covered-entities/index.html)).
- **Data scope**: HIPAA Security Rule controls apply to ePHI, while Privacy Rule obligations may apply to PHI in oral, paper, and electronic forms depending on the role and services performed. HHS describes the Security Rule as applying to electronic protected health information created, received, used, or maintained by a covered entity or business associate ([HHS Security Rule](https://www.hhs.gov/hipaa/for-professionals/security/index.html)).
- **Managed services scope**: The HIPAA boundary should include internal systems and customer-facing operations that may expose ePHI, including ticketing, RMM, EDR/MDR, SIEM, backups, remote access, privileged access tools, documentation systems, call recordings, email, file transfer, cloud consoles, logs, screenshots, diagnostic exports, and subcontractor tools.
- **No official certification**: HIPAA does not operate like ISO certification; the objective is to implement and maintain a documented compliance program that can withstand customer due diligence, contractual review, incident scrutiny, and regulatory review.
- **Legal review**: BAAs, breach-reportability decisions, state-law overlays, and contract liability terms should be reviewed by qualified privacy counsel.

## Ground-up implementation model

### Build the governance foundation

- **Assign accountability**: Appoint a HIPAA Security Officer and, if the company handles broader PHI privacy workflows, a Privacy Officer. The officer should own the HIPAA roadmap, risk register, policy set, management reporting, evidence program, incident escalation, and customer-facing compliance responses.
- **Define HIPAA service catalog**: Classify each managed service by whether it creates, receives, maintains, transmits, stores, supports, monitors, or only incidentally encounters PHI/ePHI. Examples include help desk, endpoint management, backup/DR, SOC monitoring, cloud administration, EHR support, network management, vulnerability scanning, email security, and managed detection and response.
- **Create a PHI/ePHI data-flow inventory**: Document where PHI/ePHI can enter, reside, move, or be exposed across client systems, MSP tools, logs, tickets, backups, screenshots, scripts, remote sessions, SIEM events, call recordings, and vendor platforms. HHS risk analysis guidance states that the risk analysis scope includes all ePHI an organization creates, receives, maintains, or transmits ([HHS Risk Analysis Guidance](https://www.hhs.gov/hipaa/for-professionals/security/guidance/guidance-risk-analysis/index.html)).
- **Establish compliance documentation**: Maintain a HIPAA compliance charter, control matrix, policy register, risk register, evidence index, BAA inventory, subcontractor inventory, incident register, and annual management review deck.

### Perform risk analysis and risk management

- **Conduct an accurate and thorough risk analysis**: Identify ePHI systems, assets, threats, vulnerabilities, current safeguards, likelihood, impact, and residual risk. HHS describes risk analysis as a foundational step for identifying and implementing safeguards under the Security Rule ([HHS Risk Analysis Guidance](https://www.hhs.gov/hipaa/for-professionals/security/guidance/guidance-risk-analysis/index.html)).
- **Prioritize managed-services threats**: Include ransomware, credential theft, misuse of privileged access, customer environment lateral movement, misconfigured backups, ticket attachments containing PHI, exposed logs, insecure remote administration, subcontractor compromise, misdirected support communications, and unauthorized workforce access.
- **Create a risk treatment plan**: Assign owners, target dates, remediation actions, residual risk decisions, and management approvals. For each addressable implementation specification, document whether the control is implemented, an equivalent alternative is used, or why the measure is not reasonable and appropriate.
- **Update after material changes**: Reassess risk after onboarding healthcare customers, launching a new managed service, changing RMM/PSA/SIEM tools, using AI tools, adding offshore support, introducing a new subcontractor, or materially changing backup, monitoring, or remote access architecture.

### Build policies and procedures

- **Core HIPAA policies**: Adopt policies for HIPAA governance, risk analysis, risk management, access control, workforce security, minimum necessary, acceptable use, remote access, logging and monitoring, encryption, vulnerability management, incident response, breach assessment, backup/DR, vendor management, workstation security, device/media handling, retention/disposal, and sanctions.
- **Managed-services procedures**: Add procedures for privileged customer access, emergency access, ticket handling, PHI redaction in tickets, remote session recording, screenshot restrictions, secure file exchange, customer approvals for high-risk changes, backup restore testing, log review, and customer incident notification.
- **Evidence-first design**: Every policy should map to operational evidence: access review exports, ticket samples, training logs, vendor reviews, BAA records, vulnerability reports, backup tests, incident tabletop results, and management review minutes.

### Contract and subcontractor controls

- **Execute BAAs before PHI/ePHI access**: Covered entities and business associates generally use written BAAs to obtain satisfactory assurances that PHI will be safeguarded, and HHS states that BAA terms must define permitted uses/disclosures, require safeguards, require incident and breach reporting, and address subcontractor flow-downs ([HHS Business Associate Contracts](https://www.hhs.gov/hipaa/for-professionals/covered-entities/sample-business-associate-agreement-provisions/index.html)).
- **Inventory downstream subcontractors**: Identify any tools or vendors that create, receive, maintain, transmit, support, or may access PHI/ePHI on behalf of the MSP, including cloud providers, RMM vendors, PSA/ticketing platforms, SOC platforms, backup vendors, email security vendors, AI transcription tools, call center tools, and offshore support vendors.
- **Flow down obligations**: HHS states that business associates must ensure subcontractors that create, receive, maintain, or transmit PHI agree to the same restrictions and conditions that apply to the business associate with respect to that information ([HHS Business Associate Contracts](https://www.hhs.gov/hipaa/for-professionals/covered-entities/sample-business-associate-agreement-provisions/index.html)).
- **Define customer notification windows**: HIPAA sets outer breach-notification timing rules, but managed services contracts should include operationally useful customer notification SLAs for suspected incidents, confirmed security incidents, and potential breaches.

### Implement technical and operational safeguards

- **Identity and access management**: Enforce unique user IDs, MFA, least privilege, privileged access management, just-in-time access where practical, emergency access procedures, service account governance, customer-specific access groups, and quarterly access reviews. HHS identifies access control, audit controls, integrity, authentication, and transmission security as Technical Safeguard standards ([HHS Summary of the HIPAA Security Rule](https://www.hhs.gov/hipaa/for-professionals/security/laws-regulations/index.html)).
- **Remote administration controls**: Standardize secure remote access with MFA, device posture checks, session logging, customer approval workflows for privileged changes, prohibited local credential storage, and termination procedures for support personnel.
- **Logging and monitoring**: Enable logging across RMM, PSA/ticketing, SIEM, EDR, IAM, cloud consoles, backup platforms, file transfer tools, VPN, privileged access tools, and customer admin activity. HHS explains that audit controls record and examine activity in information systems that contain or use ePHI ([HHS Technical Safeguards PDF](https://www.hhs.gov/sites/default/files/ocr/privacy/hipaa/administrative/securityrule/techsafeguards.pdf)).
- **Encryption and transmission security**: Encrypt ePHI in transit and at rest where reasonable and appropriate, protect keys, and document any compensating controls. HHS guidance for breach notification identifies encryption and destruction as technologies and methodologies for rendering PHI unusable, unreadable, or indecipherable to unauthorized persons ([HHS Unsecured PHI Guidance](https://www.hhs.gov/hipaa/for-professionals/breach-notification/guidance/index.html)).
- **Backup and contingency planning**: Maintain backup procedures, restore testing, disaster recovery plans, emergency mode operations, RPO/RTO targets, ransomware recovery playbooks, and customer-specific responsibilities.
- **Vulnerability and configuration management**: Maintain secure baselines for MSP-managed devices and internal tools, vulnerability scanning, patch SLAs, EDR coverage, anti-malware, hardening, change control, and exception tracking.

### Train workforce and operate the program

- **Train by role**: Train all workforce members on HIPAA basics, PHI handling, minimum necessary, sanctions, phishing, incident reporting, and secure remote support. Provide deeper role-based training for help desk, SOC analysts, cloud engineers, backup engineers, account managers, and executives.
- **Run tabletop exercises**: Test ransomware response, lost laptop, misdirected ticket attachment, compromised technician credentials, unauthorized access to EHR records, backup failure, and subcontractor breach scenarios.
- **Maintain evidence continuously**: Collect evidence monthly or quarterly rather than waiting for a customer audit or incident.
- **Review program annually**: Conduct annual HIPAA program review, annual risk analysis refresh, policy review, access review, vendor review, and control testing.

## First 10 actions to start next week

1. Appoint the HIPAA Security Officer and executive sponsor.
2. Create the HIPAA-scoped service catalog for all managed services.
3. Build the first PHI/ePHI data-flow map for ticketing, RMM, backup, SIEM, EDR, remote access, and customer cloud admin.
4. Inventory all healthcare customers and identify missing or outdated BAAs.
5. Inventory subcontractors and tools that may create, receive, maintain, transmit, or access PHI/ePHI.
6. Start the HIPAA risk analysis with assets, threats, vulnerabilities, current safeguards, likelihood, and impact.
7. Lock down privileged access with MFA, unique IDs, and removal of shared admin accounts.
8. Define ticketing rules for PHI minimization, secure attachments, redaction, and access restrictions.
9. Draft or refresh incident response and breach assessment procedures.
10. Create an evidence repository with folders mapped to the control matrix above.

## Practical operating cadence

- **Weekly**: Review remediation backlog, critical vulnerabilities, high-risk access changes, open incidents, and missing BAA items.
- **Monthly**: Review SIEM/log evidence, vulnerability remediation, backup job failures, sampled support tickets, access exceptions, and vendor onboarding.
- **Quarterly**: Perform access reviews, management compliance review, control evidence QA, subcontractor status review, and tabletop or focused scenario drill.
- **Annually**: Refresh HIPAA risk analysis, review policies, test disaster recovery, review vendors, update BAAs as needed, test controls, and prepare customer assurance packet.

## Recommended executive positioning

The managed services company should present HIPAA compliance as an operating program, not a one-time paperwork project. The strongest customer-facing message is: “We identify where PHI/ePHI may enter our managed services operations, maintain BAAs and subcontractor flow-downs where applicable, perform HIPAA risk analysis, enforce role-based and privileged access controls, monitor relevant activity, encrypt and back up systems, train workforce members, and maintain incident and breach assessment procedures with supporting evidence.”
